What this means

The question

A poll estimated that the fully-expanded, machine-checked proof of a basic triangle-congruence theorem, reduced all the way to ZFC set theory, would be more than 101000 steps. This project measures it directly with a small trusted proof checker and real, kernel-verified proof objects, reporting exact step counts.

Derived, not asserted

The trivial route is to assert the Birkhoff postulates (ruler, protractor, SAS, …) as axioms; then "ASA" is about 200 steps and the question is vacuous. Here the postulates are instead derived. The substrate asserts only an ordered commutative ring with 1, one extra primitive (square roots of non-negatives exist — a Euclidean field), and ordinary logic with equality. Points, lines, distance, dot product and angle congruence are conservative definitions over coordinates, never axioms. Every Birkhoff postulate used is then a kernel-verified theorem proved from that substrate.

Why there is no blow-up in the geometry

Euclidean-plane geometry, expressed through these definitions, is just polynomial identities and order facts over the substrate. SAS — the hardest postulate, needing the law of cosines, square-root-free metric reasoning, and integral-domain cancellation — comes out to about 7.25 million primitive steps when fully expanded with no shared sub-proofs at all. That is large only by everyday standards; it is around 106.9, an astronomical distance below 101000. The geometry is cheap.

Where the large numbers actually live

The only place a genuinely large number appears is in constructing the real numbers from sets. When the substrate axioms are discharged against set.mm's fully constructed real field, the cost is about 1045.7, dominated by the existence of square roots routed through analytic completeness machinery. Completeness itself is never used by the geometry; the expense is the construction of the field, not any deep theorem about it. A leaner model in which square roots are a primitive of the field brings the substrate term down to about 1037. Either way the result is nowhere near 101000: the poll over-estimates by roughly 950 orders of magnitude.

The same theorem is valid in the real reals

The grounded proof never uses any property beyond the substrate axioms — no completeness, no Archimedean property, no particular construction. So it holds in every Euclidean field: the real numbers, the real algebraic numbers, real-closed fields, the computable reals, and so on. Logically, the substrate axioms are all theorems of the real field, so the proof transfers upward to the standard real numbers unchanged; the minimal algebraic model is enough to establish validity, and the expensive real construction inherits exactly the same theorem. The cheap proof is the portable one — a proof that leaned on completeness would not transfer down.

Reading the sizes

Two numbers matter. The inlined size is the cut-free count with no sub-proof reuse at all — a deliberately pessimistic metric. The shared size is the same proof kept as a directed acyclic graph with common sub-proofs shared once; it is far smaller and is what a real proof library stores. Both are exact and re-checked by the kernel.
